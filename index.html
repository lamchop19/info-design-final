Era I · The Pioneer Era 1903 – 1939
No. 01

Maurice Garin · 1903

Portrait — Garin

A chimney sweep turned racing legend, Maurice Garin won the inaugural Tour de France with a commanding lead across all six stages. Born in Italy and naturalised French, he completed the 2,428-kilometre course through gravel roads and summer dust at an average speed that stunned a nation still becoming accustomed to the bicycle. His victory cemented the race as an institution.

Garin's form was so dominant in that first edition that he finished nearly three hours ahead of the next man — a margin that has never been matched again. The Tour was conceived as a publicity stunt for a newspaper; Garin turned it into legend. He was disqualified from the 1904 edition for taking a train, and retired from professional racing not long after. The race, however, was just beginning.

Era I · The Pioneer Era 1903 – 1939
No. 02

Octave Lapize · 1910

Portrait — Lapize

Nicknamed Le Frisé — The Curly One — Octave Lapize won the 1910 Tour in the year the race first crossed the high Pyrenees. Ascending the Col du Tourmalet on a single-speed bicycle with a fixed gear, he famously screamed at race officials standing at the summit: "Vous êtes des assassins!" The organisers, undeterred, returned the following year with even steeper climbs.

A Parisian by birth and an amateur boxer in his off-season, Lapize combined raw power with tactical aggression to conquer a field of 110 starters. His victory represented a turning point: the Tour was no longer merely a road race — it had become a test of elemental endurance in the mountains. He died in 1917, shot down as a fighter pilot over Verdun at 29.

Era II · The Golden Age 1940 – 1965
No. 03

Fausto Coppi · 1949

Portrait — Coppi

Il Campionissimo. Fausto Coppi was the greatest Italian cyclist who ever lived — a man so gifted that rivals competed not for victory but for second. In 1949 he completed the unprecedented Giro–Tour double, winning both Grand Tours in the same calendar year with a style so fluid that journalists struggled for words.

His war with Gino Bartali — secular versus sacred, industrial North versus rural South — divided all of Italy. Coppi's angular build and radical dropped posture on the bicycle prefigured aerodynamics research by forty years. He died in 1960 from misdiagnosed malaria contracted in Africa. He was forty years old. The sport has not seen anyone quite like him before or since.

Era II · The Golden Age 1940 – 1965
No. 04

Jacques Anquetil · 1961

Portrait — Anquetil

The first man to win five Tours, Jacques Anquetil from Normandy was cycling's first genuine superstar — handsome, aloof, and possessed of the most precise time-trial position the sport had seen. He won the 1961 Tour from start to finish, holding the yellow jersey every single day of the race, a feat matched by almost no one before or since.

Anquetil raced on his own terms. He was known to eat lobster and drink champagne the night before critical time trials, and he openly admitted to doping in an era when it was considered a professional tool. His five victories between 1957 and 1964 made him the standard against which all who followed were measured — at least until a Belgian from Brussels arrived and made the benchmark unreachable.

Era III · The Colossus Era 1966 – 1985
No. 05

Eddy Merckx · 1969

Portrait — Merckx

The Cannibal. No rider in the history of the Tour — or professional cycling — has come close to what Eddy Merckx achieved in 1969. He won six stages, the mountains classification, the points classification, and the overall title with a margin of nearly eighteen minutes. He was 24 years old, and it was his Tour debut.

The Belgian from Brussels won five Tours in total, attacking not when tactically necessary but always — consuming every race with an appetite that left rivals and commentators equally speechless. His record of 34 Tour stage victories stood for over fifty years. Racing against Merckx, wrote one journalist, felt like competing against a force of nature that simply hadn't been told the rules applied to it.

Era III · The Colossus Era 1966 – 1985
No. 06

Bernard Hinault · 1978

Portrait — Hinault

Le Blaireau — The Badger. Bernard Hinault from Brittany won five Tours over eight years with a combative, attacking style that earned him a fierce popular following across France. His first victory in 1978 came after he organised a rider strike at Valence-d'Agen over race start times — an act of militant solidarity that told you everything about the man.

He remains the last Frenchman to win his home race, and has reportedly vowed not to attend the final podium until one of his countrymen does. Hinault fought with teammates, rivals, and journalists with equal ferocity. His battles with Greg LeMond in 1986 — ostensibly the year he was helping the American win — became one of the sport's most contested chapters. He disputes it still.

Era IV · The Precision Era 1986 – 2005
No. 07

Greg LeMond · 1986

Portrait — LeMond

Greg LeMond was the first American to win the Tour de France and the man who quietly rewrote how the sport thought about technology. He raced with aerodynamic triathlon bars and a streamlined helmet, deployed a heart rate monitor as a pacing tool, and treated cycling as a measurable science at a time when most riders trained by feel.

His 1989 victory remains the most dramatic finish in Tour history. He entered the final day's time trial trailing Laurent Fignon by fifty seconds. He gained 58 seconds over 24.5 kilometres in Paris — winning by eight seconds, the closest margin ever recorded. LeMond's career was interrupted by a near-fatal hunting accident that left shotgun pellets lodged near his heart. That he won at all after that — let alone three times — remains one of sport's most improbable stories.

No. 08

Miguel Indurain · 1991

Portrait — Indurain

Big Mig. The gentle giant from Navarra won five consecutive Tours from 1991 to 1995 — a record that stood until Armstrong — with a style that appeared almost passionless to watch and was utterly devastating in practice. He never attacked in the mountains. He simply refused to crack, sitting within the lead group with a resting heart rate reportedly as low as 28 bpm, and then obliterating every rival in the time trials.

His VO₂ max was measured at 88 ml/kg/min, among the highest ever recorded in any athlete in any sport. Indurain's five consecutive victories were won with the quiet, mechanical efficiency of a precision instrument — a profound contrast to every champion who came before, and most who have followed since.

Era V · The Modern Era 2006 – Present
No. 09

Lance Armstrong · 1999

Portrait — Armstrong

Seven consecutive Tour victories. A comeback from testicular cancer that spread to his brain and lungs. Livestrong wristbands worn by 80 million people across the world. And then, in August 2012, a USADA investigation concluded the most sophisticated doping programme in the history of professional sport — stripping Armstrong of all seven titles and issuing a lifetime ban.

The Armstrong story is the Tour's most complex and contested: a man whose survival was genuine, whose willpower was extraordinary, and whose victories were built on systematic fraud. The seven-year span he dominated — 1999 to 2005 — remains officially without a winner. His 2013 television confession to Oprah Winfrey, laconic and calculated, resolved nothing. The debate about his era, and about what sport owes its myths, continues.

Era V · The Modern Era 2006 – Present
No. 10

Tadej Pogačar · 2020

Portrait — Pogačar

The Slovenian prodigy arrived at the 2020 Tour as a 21-year-old with a GoPro mounted to his helmet, grinning on every climb. He left as the youngest champion since Henri Cornet in 1904. His penultimate-day time trial — overturning a 57-second deficit to his compatriot Primož Roglič in under 36 kilometres — was among the most extraordinary individual performances in the race's history.

Pogačar's style is reminiscent of Merckx in its total aggression: he attacks in the mountains, wins the time trials, contests the sprints, and occasionally wins stages that no GC rider has any business contesting. In an era defined by data, watts per kilogram, and cold calculation, he races with a joy that seems almost irrational — and which has revived something essential about what the race is supposed to be.
